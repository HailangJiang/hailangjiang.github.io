Hi! I’m Hailang Jiang [ˈhaɪ.lɑŋ d͡ʒɑŋ], a first-year PhD student in the Linguistics Dept at Harvard University. I specialize in phonology; I’m also studying data science. Feel free to reach out to me at first name last name [æt] g.harvard.edu.

Previously, I obtained my master's degree in linguistics from University College London and my bachelor’s degree in economics from Peking University.


My research interests includes phonotactics, experimental/computational methods, morphophonology, productivity/learnability and phonetics-phonology interface.

I don’t work on any specific languages, but I have worked on Japanese, Mandarin, and some artificial languages. See here for my latest studies (any advice and comments are welcomed).

  • Learnability of length-referencing alternation in Japanese and an artificial language.
    The unnatural alternation of Japanese /g/ nasalization conditioned by mora length is not internalized by native speakers, as evidenced by a wug test extending this pattern to novel words. [abstract and slides for AMP2023] A structure akin to it is also examined in an artificial language learning experiment, which demonstrates a universal learning bias against referencing length, or ‘counting’. [abstract for OCP21]
  • Utterance-final high vowel diphthongization in Chongqing Mandarin.
    I analyze vowels such as /i#/[iɪ#] and /u#/[uʊ#] as the results of the interaction of two independent constraints: final lengthening and *HighHigh. [abstract and slides for MFM30] To predict the inventory of diphthongization processes of this kind, I am currently employing dispersion theory. [abstract]
